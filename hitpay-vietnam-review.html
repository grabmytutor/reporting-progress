HitPay has a specific policy on businesses that both collect payments AND pay out to third parties (e.g., tutors). Platforms fitting this model may be classified as higher-risk and subject to the Merchant Monitoring Programme — requiring a 20–50% reserve hold on card volume.

What the Checkout Actually Looks Like for a Vietnamese Student

❌

MoMo user — cannot pay

A student who primarily uses MoMo (Vietnam's #1 wallet) opens the checkout page. HitPay does not offer MoMo as a payment option. The student sees no familiar payment method and abandons the purchase.

❌

Napas card holder — cannot pay

Most Vietnamese bank cards are domestic Napas debit cards, not international Visa/Mastercard. HitPay does not accept Napas cards. Students who have never applied for an international card are blocked entirely.

⚡

VietQR user — can pay (partial win)

A student with a Vietnamese bank account can scan a VietQR code to transfer money directly from their bank. This is a genuine pathway — but it is slower and less frictionless than wallet payments, and not all students will know how to use it.

💳

International card holder — can pay

A student with a Visa or Mastercard (often from a premium bank account) can pay. However, this demographic is a minority among Vietnamese students and young adults — typically urban, higher-income families.

Estimated checkout coverage: Based on Vietnamese payment behaviour data, roughly 70–80% of Vietnamese student-age consumers would encounter no viable payment method at a HitPay checkout (no MoMo, no ZaloPay, no VNPay, no Napas card). Only ~20–30% — those with international cards or who know how to use VietQR bank transfer — could complete a payment. This directly translates to lost revenue and poor conversion rates in Vietnam.

Hidden Cost: Merchant Monitoring Programme

The reserve hold risk: If HitPay's systems flag EdBuddies as higher-risk — which is more likely for a marketplace model that collects and disburses funds — the Merchant Monitoring Programme can be triggered. This imposes:

• A 2% MDR surcharge on top of standard transaction rates
• A mandatory cash reserve of 20–50% of average monthly card payment volume, held for up to 4 months
• Payouts may be suspended while the review is ongoing, even while the account can still accept payments

For an EdTech marketplace handling tutor payouts, a 4-month hold on 20–50% of card volume would be a serious cash-flow problem. This policy should be disclosed and discussed with HitPay before scaling card payment volume.

⚠️ Key Business Risks for EdBuddies Using HitPay in Vietnam

Risks ranked by severity — the top three are fundamental structural gaps, not operational issues that can be contractually resolved.

🚨 No SBV Licence — Regulatory Non-Compliance
Critical

Vietnam's payment regulations (Circular 39 and its 2025 updates) require any payment intermediary processing payments in Vietnam to hold an SBV licence. HitPay holds no such licence and has no local Vietnamese entity. Accepting VND payments from Vietnamese customers through an unlicensed foreign gateway creates potential regulatory exposure — both for HitPay and for EdBuddies as its merchant. Confirm legal opinion on this before scaling Vietnam payment volume through HitPay.

🚨 Missing Core Vietnam Payment Methods
Critical

MoMo, ZaloPay, VNPay, and Napas ATM cards are not supported. These cover an estimated 70–80% of payment method usage among Vietnamese student-age consumers. Without them, the majority of EdBuddies' Vietnam target users cannot pay. This is not a configuration issue — it is a fundamental product gap that HitPay cannot resolve quickly, as supporting these wallets requires Vietnamese regulatory partnerships that do not exist yet.

🚨 Vietnam Entity Cannot Onboard
Critical

If EdBuddies expands with a Vietnam-registered legal entity (which may be required under Vietnamese law for operating a platform that collects payments from Vietnamese users), that entity cannot open a HitPay merchant account. HitPay does not support Vietnam as a merchant registration country. The business would need a separate payment provider for its Vietnam entity regardless.

Account Freeze / Payout Suspension Risk
High

Multiple verified merchant reports describe accounts having payouts suspended during compliance reviews, sometimes for weeks or months. HitPay's Merchant Monitoring Programme can freeze payouts while still allowing payment acceptance — meaning EdBuddies would collect revenue from students that it cannot access. For a marketplace that needs to pay tutors regularly, this is an operational catastrophe risk.

Reserve Hold for Marketplace Model
High

EdBuddies' business model — collecting from students and disbursing to tutors — is classified as higher-risk by most payment processors. HitPay's Merchant Monitoring Programme may trigger a 20–50% cash reserve requirement on monthly card volume, held for up to 4 months. At $50,000/month in card volume, this could lock up $10,000–$25,000 in reserves — a meaningful cash-flow drain for a growing startup.

No Vietnamese-Language Operations
Medium

The merchant dashboard, all documentation, and customer support are English-only. A Vietnam operations team would need English proficiency to use HitPay effectively. More practically, any payment-related issue experienced by a Vietnamese student cannot be escalated in Vietnamese — creating a customer support dead-end.

Company Scale and Product Velocity
Medium

HitPay last raised funds in May 2022. It has fewer resources than competitors like Airwallex or 2C2P to invest in Vietnam market development. MoMo, ZaloPay, and VNPay integrations require local regulatory agreements — progress may be slow. EdBuddies should not wait for HitPay to add Vietnam payment methods as a strategy.

Opaque FX Pricing on VND Conversions
Low

HitPay does not publish its FX spread for currency conversion. When Vietnamese students pay in VND and EdBuddies settles in SGD, the conversion happens at an undisclosed rate. At low volume this is immaterial, but at scale the spread could become a meaningful cost versus providers with published 0.5–1% FX rates.

📌 What Should EdBuddies Do?

A clear-eyed action plan based on whether Vietnam is a core or peripheral market for EdBuddies.

The honest summary: HitPay is not the right primary gateway for Vietnam. It should continue to serve EdBuddies' Singapore operations where it works well — but Vietnam requires a complementary solution. The question is not whether to add a Vietnam-capable gateway, but which one and how quickly.
1

Keep HitPay for Singapore — do not replace it

HitPay works well for EdBuddies' Singapore operations: payment links, invoicing, PayNow, Xero sync. It is cost-effective and the team already knows it. Do not disrupt what works. The goal is to extend capability for Vietnam, not do a full platform migration.

2

Immediately evaluate a Vietnam-capable gateway as a parallel integration

EdBuddies needs MoMo, ZaloPay, VNPay, and VietQR for Vietnamese students. The two strongest candidates are: (a) Airwallex — licensed in Vietnam (via CTIN Pay), supports MoMo and VND bank transfer, handles cross-border FX well; and (b) VNPay/PayOS — Vietnam-native, full local wallet coverage, lowest VND transaction fees but no cross-border capability. A combined approach (Airwallex for cards/cross-border + VNPay for local wallets) may be optimal.

3

Get legal clarity on the regulatory position of VietQR-only collection

Before scaling VND collection through HitPay's VietQR integration, get a legal opinion on whether a Singapore-registered entity accepting VND payments from Vietnamese users via an unlicensed-in-Vietnam gateway creates any regulatory exposure under SBV rules. This is a compliance question, not a technical one — it requires a Vietnamese legal firm with fintech expertise.

4

Confirm HitPay's VietQR fee structure in writing

HitPay has not published pricing for VietQR transactions on its standard pricing page. Before routing any significant Vietnamese student volume through VietQR, request the exact fee schedule in writing from HitPay support. Also confirm settlement currency and FX rate model for VND → SGD conversions.

5

Negotiate payout protection terms before scaling card volume

If EdBuddies grows card payment volume on HitPay, the risk of triggering the Merchant Monitoring Programme (and associated reserve holds) increases. Before this happens, negotiate with HitPay for: (a) advance notice before any payout restriction, (b) a maximum reserve hold percentage and duration, and (c) a named escalation contact. Get these in writing or in a signed addendum to the merchant agreement.
